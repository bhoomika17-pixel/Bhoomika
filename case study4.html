Accessibility Research

Improving Link Legibility and Accessibility Across Enterprise Experiences

Mixed-method research resolving a high-stakes accessibility decision through controlled evaluation and inclusive feedback.

Role & Scope

  • person UX Research Lead
  • checklist Controlled A/B Evaluation
  • analytics Statistical ANOVA Analysis
  • visibility Inclusive Accessibility Testing

Context

A recent link underline update triggered legibility concerns, leading some teams to consider deviating from the standard. I led mixed-method research to evaluate underline treatments, demonstrating that increased offset improved readability for dyslexic and low-vision users. This provided the evidence-based guide needed to standardize link styling across 230+ service offerings.

Approach

Controlled Evaluation

Quantitative readability and recognition measures of multiple link underline treatments.

Qualitative Feedback

Gathered insights on scanning efficiency, clarity, and visual comfort from diverse users.

Inclusive Recruits

Focused participation including individuals with dyslexia and low-vision conditions.

Key Insights

auto_stories

Minimal spacing increases reading effort.

Participants with dyslexia reported that tight underlines made characters harder to distinguish. Increased spacing between the underline and letterforms improved recognition.

done_all

No Usability Tradeoffs

While performance for users without disabilities was consistent, the increased offset improved visual comfort across the board.

analytics

Outcomes vs. Perception

Aesthetic concerns regarding "visual heaviness" did not align with actual readability results, which favored the increased offset.
